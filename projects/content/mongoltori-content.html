Project Overview

BRACU Mongol-Tori is a pioneering Mars rover project developed by BRAC University's robotics team. The name "Mongol-Tori" comes from the Bengali words for "Mars" (Mongol) and "Boat" (Tori), symbolizing our journey to explore the red planet.

Comprehending the significance of teamwork and cooperation in the voyage of discovering greatness, the team has successfully combined their skills and efforts to build a rover capable of aiding inquisitive observers to discover the unknown and perform science-related verification tasks.

The project is led by a group of tech enthusiasts from different departments, mostly consisting of engineering students with BRAC University's Robotic Club (ROBU) serving as a parent organization for financial and mechanical engineering support.

Key Features

The BRACU Mongol-Tori rover incorporates several advanced features that enable it to perform complex tasks in simulated Martian environments. Each system was carefully designed to meet the challenging requirements of international rover competitions.

Specialized Chassis

Our H-shaped chassis (0.7m long and 0.55m wide) features triangulation to reduce deformation while traversing rocky terrain. The heavy space frame transmits flexing loads as tension and compression along each strut, providing stability in challenging conditions.

Articulated Arm

A strong articulated arm with six degrees of freedom (base, shoulder, elbow, wrist) and two degrees of freedom in the end effector. Features feedback actuators for implementing inverse kinematics algorithms and a worm gear system that locks if the motor fails.

Advanced Suspension

Our modified rocker-bogie suspension system with four wheels includes two bogies directly connected to the chassis and supported by a U-shaped differential bar. The system can expand from 0.76m to 1.2m using heavy-duty feedback actuators for vertical drops and slopes.

Communication System

Utilizes a peer-to-peer network with 2.4GHz routers for seamless data flow. The base station uses a Ubiquiti Rocket M2 with a 15dBi directional sector antenna, while the rover has a Ubiquiti Bullet M2 with a 12dBi omnidirectional antenna for 360Â° coverage up to 1km.

Science Module

Our integrated science module can collect soil samples and analyze them for signs of life. It includes environmental sensors (temperature, humidity, gases), biomass testing, water capillary testing, and specialized spectroscopy for analyzing soil composition.

Technical Systems

Mechanical Design

The rover's mechanical design has been optimized through several iterations. The chassis evolved from a K-shape to an H-shape design, measuring 0.7m long and 0.55m wide. The triangulation installed in the chassis reduces deformation during rough terrain traversal by transmitting loads as tension and compression along each strut.

The wheels are 0.3m in diameter and 0.10m wide, constructed with a stainless steel ring via U-shaped rim. To reduce weight while maintaining strength, small holes were drilled in the wheels. Rubber pads are used on the wheels for better grip when climbing rocks, performing 360-degree rotation, and handling vertical drops.

A unique feature is the extender system that increases the length of the rover's base from 0.76m to 1.2m, preventing it from flipping over during vertical drops and providing greater stability on steep slopes.

Control Systems

The rover uses an ATMega2560 microcontroller to process commands. For wheel control, back EMF protected 30A motor drivers provide smooth PWM operation. The arm uses 13A motor drivers and 10A relay modules for precise movements. A reverse polarity protection circuit using Schottky diodes protects against unexpected errors.

The control station features a custom GUI built in Java that follows TCP/IP protocol, with the base station acting as the client and the rover as the server. A feedback system allows for precise control of feedback end effectors. For science operations, a separate GUI provides real-time sensor readings with graphical visualization.

Autonomous Navigation

For autonomous traversal, the rover employs a Pixhawk 4.0 with ardurover firmware and a Radiolink SE100 GPS-Compass module, achieving positioning accuracy within 50cm. The "Mission Planner" interface allows for waypoint setting, holding positions, and creating search patterns using loiter mode.

Object detection is handled by an Rplidar that rotates 360 degrees, firing 150,000 laser pulses per second. AR tag recognition uses image processing with OpenCV through contour detection and homography, with an accuracy of approximately 80%.

Power System

The power system has evolved from separate batteries to a centralized power distribution system. The 2021 design uses five 8000mAh Lithium Jump Starter batteries, chosen for ease of charging, portability, and flight security clearance. This configuration provides an average runtime of 48 minutes.

Earlier designs used 10,000mAh Li-Po batteries (2019) and later six 10,000mAh batteries (2020) with an average run time of 45 minutes. A high-ampere kill switch provides emergency shutdown capability in line with competition safety requirements.

Science Module

The science module has gone through significant evolution across iterations, focusing on analyzing soil samples for signs of life and water history. The system is divided into three main components: a digging module and sample collector, an environmental data collection sensor box, and an automatic onboard laboratory.

Environmental Sensors

The sensor array includes air temperature and humidity sensors (DHT22), soil temperature sensors (DS18B20), soil moisture sensors, gas sensors (CO, CO2, H2), light intensity measurement, UV radiation detection, magnetic field sensors, and an endoscopic camera for trench visualization.

Water Capillary Test

Utilizes a centrifugal tube to hold soil and a servo-controlled syringe to supply water. This test indicates whether the soil surface holds water (as surface water) or absorbs and stores it (as groundwater), providing insights into the soil's history and composition.

Spectroscopic Analysis

The 2021 model introduced Raman spectroscopy for more accurate soil analysis. This quantitative technique uses light sources and sensors to measure light absorbance, determining the presence of crucial elements like Nitrogen, Potassium, and Phosphorus in soil samples.

The sample collection system can extract around 80-100 grams of soil per grab using an excavator claw that can dig up to 20cm deep. For amino acid detection, ninhydrin reagent is used with a heat generator to identify potential biomarkers. The system also includes digital microscopy for examining soil composition at magnifications up to 800x.

Testing & Operations

Each iteration of the rover underwent extensive testing procedures to ensure reliable performance in competition conditions. The team follows a three-step testing protocol:

Subsystem Testing

Individual components are first evaluated using Solidworks simulation before physical implementation. Subsystems including wheels, suspension, chassis, end effectors, and the sample collector are tested separately. Custom test fixtures, such as ramps for wheel testing and object manipulation trials for arm testing, are used to validate specific functionalities.

Complete System Testing

After assembly, full system integration tests evaluate how components work together. Communication systems, electronic configurations, robotic arm operation, and end effector functionality are tested as a complete unit. The team verifies that all systems can maintain reliable operation for the expected mission duration.

Operational Testing

Final testing replicates competition conditions at remote locations with terrain similar to the Mars Desert Research Station. The team conducts trials for each competition task: Extreme Retrieval and Delivery, Equipment Servicing, Autonomous Traversal, and Science Task. Each operation is performed within time constraints to simulate competition pressure.

Future Directions

The BRACU Mongol-Tori project continues to evolve with each iteration, with several development areas planned for future versions:

  • Enhanced autonomous capabilities with advanced machine learning algorithms for more adaptive terrain navigation
  • Expanded scientific analysis capabilities, including more sophisticated sample analysis tools
  • Further refinement of the cartesian arm design for improved precision in sample collection
  • Integration of AI-based object recognition to improve target identification during missions
  • Optimization of power management systems to extend operational duration

This project has not only produced a successful rover prototype but has also provided invaluable hands-on experience for students interested in robotics, space exploration, and interdisciplinary engineering challenges. The experience gained through international competitions continues to inform future designs and educational opportunities at BRAC University.
